# Problem Signals: haru-gratitude-diary

## 1. Core User Problems -- Why People Fail at Gratitude Journaling

### P1. Forgetting to Write (Habit Collapse)
The most common reason people quit journaling is not boredom -- it is simply forgetting. Without a strong contextual trigger anchored to an existing routine, the habit never forms neural pathways strong enough to self-sustain. Users report that after 5-7 days of enthusiasm, the app notification becomes invisible noise, no different from any other badge on the phone.

### P2. Repetition Fatigue ("I already wrote coffee and sunshine yesterday")
The brain's dopamine production plateaus and then declines as the neural track for a habit is laid down. After the first week, users feel they are writing the same three things every day: good weather, a meal, a kind message. The journal starts to feel mechanical rather than meaningful. This is the single biggest driver of abandonment between day 7 and day 30.

### P3. Forced Positivity on Bad Days
On genuinely difficult days -- grief, burnout, conflict -- forcing gratitude feels dishonest. Users describe it as "toxic positivity in app form." Rather than skip a day and break the streak, many users quit entirely. The app punishes honesty by design.

### P4. Not Knowing What to Write (Blank Page Anxiety)
Open-ended prompts ("What are you grateful for?") cause decision fatigue. Users stare at the empty text field for 30 seconds, feel inadequate, and close the app. The problem is especially acute for men, who report that "complex prompts needing quiet reflection" do not fit their daily rhythm.

### P5. No Visible Payoff (Effort Without Feedback)
Users invest emotional labor daily but never see a return. There is no "aha moment" where accumulated entries reveal a pattern, shift a mood, or prove that life is better than it felt. The journal is write-only -- entries go into a void and are never surfaced again in a meaningful way.

### P6. Feature Bloat in Existing Apps
Leading gratitude apps bundle vision boards, affirmations, mood trackers, habit trackers, and social features. Users who want "just a gratitude journal" feel overwhelmed. One reviewer complained about "way too many things so you can only access through premium." The core journaling experience is buried under upsells.

## 2. Jobs-To-Be-Done Hypotheses

### JTBD-1: The Evening Wind-Down
**When** I am lying in bed scrolling my phone before sleep,
**I want to** quickly capture three good things from today,
**so I can** shift my mental state from anxious rumination to calm reflection and fall asleep more easily.

### JTBD-2: The Rough Day Recovery
**When** I have had a terrible, stressful, or emotionally draining day,
**I want to** find even one small thing that was not awful,
**so I can** break the cognitive loop of "everything is bad" and regain a sense of proportion.

### JTBD-3: The Weekly Pattern Recognition
**When** I feel like nothing is changing or improving in my life,
**I want to** look back at a week of gratitude entries and see recurring themes,
**so I can** realize what actually matters to me and feel a sense of quiet progress.

### JTBD-4: The Habit Restart
**When** I have tried gratitude journaling before and quit after two weeks,
**I want to** start again with zero guilt and minimal friction,
**so I can** build the habit without the shame of past failure killing my motivation.

### JTBD-5: The Therapy Homework
**When** my therapist or counselor recommends I start a gratitude practice,
**I want to** have a structured, simple tool that does exactly that and nothing else,
**so I can** follow through on the recommendation without downloading a lifestyle app I did not ask for.

### JTBD-6: The Parenting Anchor
**When** I want to model gratitude for my children at dinner or bedtime,
**I want to** quickly show or read back recent entries as conversation starters,
**so I can** make gratitude a family ritual rather than a private chore.

## 3. Desperate Specificity -- What Makes the Pain Acute Enough to Switch

### Why pen-and-paper fails:
- Physical journals get lost, forgotten at home, or feel like "one more thing to carry."
- You cannot search old entries. The gratitude you wrote three months ago is effectively gone.
- There is no weekly summary. No one flips back through 30 pages to count patterns.
- On bad days, a blank notebook page feels more intimidating than a phone screen.

### Why existing apps fail:
- **Gratitude (Gratefulness.me):** Beautiful but bloated. Vision boards, affirmations, community features, premium gates. Users who want minimalism get a lifestyle platform.
- **365 Gratitude:** AI coaching and guided challenges add complexity. Users report the app "tries to do too much."
- **Day One / generic journal apps:** Not purpose-built for gratitude. Three-item structure is not enforced. No weekly reflection. It is just a blank page with a date stamp.
- **Gratitude Plus:** Paywalled features frustrate users. "Too many things locked behind premium."

### The acute switching trigger:
The user who will switch is not looking for "a better journal." They are looking for relief from complexity. They opened an existing app, saw 6 tabs they did not need, a subscription prompt, and an AI chatbot -- and thought: "I just want to write three things and see my week. Why is this so hard?" That moment of frustration is the entry point.

## 4. User Signals from Competitor App Store Reviews

### Signal cluster: "I wanted simple, I got a platform"
- "There's way too many things so you can only access through premium." (Gratitude Plus review)
- "The affirmations tab is extremely cluttered and disorganized -- 20+ personal folders mixed with 5 dozen system folders." (Gratitude app review)
- "I dread looking at the app and use it less as a result." (Gratitude app review on UI clutter)
- Gratitude Garden praised specifically for "the perfect amount of structure and simplicity to make keeping a daily journal not feel overwhelming."

### Signal cluster: "Design choices that break my flow"
- "The journal background was changed from black to white -- the white background is jarring to my eyes." (Gratitude app, multiple reviewers)
- Crashes reported when writing entries from nudge cards.
- Date picker swipes away while adjusting the date.
- Text overlapping while writing journal entries.
- December 2025 entries incorrectly displayed as year 2026.

### Signal cluster: "I stopped and came back"
- Multiple reviewers describe stopping for months, then restarting. This confirms that the desire for gratitude journaling persists even when the habit breaks. The problem is not motivation -- it is friction and format.

### Signal cluster: Korean market specifics
- Korean users praise "euneunan pastel-ton" (subtle pastel tones) and clean screen composition.
- 50-character limit option valued for days when users want brevity.
- Lack of edit/copy-paste functionality frustrates users in Korean gratitude apps.

### Synthesis:
Users want three things the market under-delivers on: (1) radical simplicity, (2) a weekly lookback that makes daily entries feel worthwhile, (3) graceful handling of days they skip or struggle.

## 5. Emotional Triggers -- What Moments Lead Someone to START a Gratitude Practice

### Trigger 1: Post-Crisis Clarity
After a health scare, job loss, breakup, or death of someone close. The brush with loss makes ordinary life feel precious. The person searches "gratitude journal" at 11 PM, downloads an app, and writes their first entry with tears in their eyes. This is the highest-intent moment.

### Trigger 2: Therapy or Self-Help Recommendation
A therapist says "try writing three things you are grateful for each night." A book (Atomic Habits, The Happiness Advantage) or podcast recommends it. The user is not self-motivated -- they are following an authority's advice. They need the app to match exactly what was prescribed: simple, three items, daily.

### Trigger 3: Seasonal / New Year Reset
January 1, March (new school year in Korea), birthday, or start of a new job. The psychological fresh start triggers a burst of self-improvement intent. Gratitude journaling is low-cost, low-commitment, and feels virtuous. The challenge is surviving past the 2-week enthusiasm window.

### Trigger 4: Burnout and Negativity Spiral
The user notices they have been complaining constantly, doom-scrolling, or snapping at family. They do not want therapy yet. They want a small, private intervention. Gratitude journaling is the "minimum viable mental health practice." The trigger is self-disgust, not aspiration.

### Trigger 5: Pandemic / Global Anxiety Aftereffects
The COVID-19 pandemic caused a 25% spike in depression and anxiety cases globally in its first year. This generated sustained interest in gratitude as a low-barrier therapeutic tool. The trigger is ambient dread -- the feeling that the world is getting worse and the need to consciously counter it.

### Trigger 6: Seeing Someone Else's Practice
A friend mentions their gratitude journal. A social media post shows a filled-in weekly page. The user thinks: "That looks easy. I should do that." The trigger is social proof combined with perceived low effort. The gap between "that looks easy" and "this is actually hard to maintain" is where most users fall.

## Discovery Status

**Validated (high confidence from research):**
- Repetition fatigue and forgetting are the two dominant failure modes.
- Existing apps over-index on features; minimalism is under-served.
- Weekly reflection / lookback is desired but rarely well-implemented.
- Korean users value pastel aesthetics, clean composition, and brevity options.

**Partially validated (needs primary research):**
- Whether "3 items only" constraint helps or frustrates (some days users want more).
- Whether weekly summary email/notification is a stronger retention lever than streaks.
- Whether prompt variety (different questions each day) solves repetition fatigue or adds friction.

**Unvalidated (requires user interviews):**
- Willingness to pay for a minimalist gratitude app vs. expectation that simple = free.
- Whether the "rough day" use case (JTBD-2) is real or aspirational.
- Family/shared gratitude use case demand.

**Recommended next experiment:**
Run 5 user interviews targeting people who have downloaded and then abandoned a gratitude app within the past 6 months. Focus on the moment of abandonment: what happened the last day they used it, and what they wish the app had done differently. Use the interview script skill to generate the guide.
